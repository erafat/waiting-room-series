# Screen Copy

Use this copy in the mobile prototype and future production builds. Keep the text outside the generated images.

## 01. Hero

**Headline**
Your First Seizure Doctor Visit

**Deck**
What to expect, step by step.

**Body**
Feeling nervous is normal. This guide walks you through the visit in plain language so the appointment feels less unfamiliar.

## 02. Check-In

**Headline**
First: checking in

**Body**
When you arrive, clinic staff help you get checked in and point you in the right direction. You may be asked to confirm your appointment and review forms or history information.

**Support Line**
Keep your medicines, records, and questions easy to reach.

## 03. While You Wait

**Headline**
While you wait

**Body**
This is a good moment to gather the details you want to share.

**Checklist**
- what happened before, during, and after the event
- medicines you take now
- questions you want answered
- a family member or friend who saw what happened

## 04. Meet The Doctor

**Headline**
Meet your seizure doctor

**Body**
An epileptologist is a neurologist who specializes in seizures. The visit is a conversation, not a test you can fail.

**Quote**
"I'm here to listen and work with you."

## 05. Tell Your Story

**Headline**
Your story matters

**Body**
Your doctor will ask what you felt, what other people saw, how long it lasted, and how you felt afterward. Even small details can help.

**Support Line**
If a family member or friend witnessed the event, their description can be useful too.

## 06. Before, During, After

**Headline**
Before, during, after

**Body**
The sequence matters. If there was a warning feeling before the event, what happened during it, and how you felt afterward can all help your doctor understand the episode.

## 07. Which Details Matter

**Headline**
Which details matter

**Body**
You do not need perfect memory. Just share the parts you remember and ask family or friends to help fill in what they saw.

**Three Parts**
- Before: any warning feeling such as a strange smell, taste, rising feeling, deja vu, fear, or a sudden "something is wrong" feeling
- During: what you did and what other people saw, such as staring, shaking, stiffening, falling, or not responding
- After: how long it took to recover, and whether you felt confused, exhausted, sore, or had trouble speaking

**Note**
Not everyone has all three. Share the parts that happened to you.

## 08. What May Happen Today

**Headline**
What may happen today

**Body**
The first visit often includes a careful history, a neurologic exam, and a discussion about next steps. Your doctor may talk about tests such as an EEG, MRI, or blood work.

**Note**
Not every test happens the same day.

## 09. Making Sense Of It

**Headline**
One seizure does not automatically mean epilepsy

**Body**
Part of this visit is understanding what happened and what needs to be checked next. The goal is clarity, not rushing to conclusions.

## 10. Questions Are Welcome

**Headline**
Bring your questions

**Body**
Questions help you leave with a clearer plan.

**Question Prompts**
- Can I drive?
- What should I do if this happens again?
- Do I need testing right away?
- When should I call the clinic?
- What should my family watch for?

## 11. Before You Leave

**Headline**
Leave with a plan

**Body**
Before you go, you should understand the next step: testing, medicines if needed, safety guidance, and follow-up.

**Quote**
"You came in with questions. You should leave knowing what happens next."

## 12. Closing

**Headline**
You do not have to figure this out alone

**Body**
Bring questions. Bring support. Ask for clarity. This visit is the start of a plan, not the end of the story.
