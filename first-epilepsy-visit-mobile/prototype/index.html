02 / Arrival

First: checking in

When you arrive, clinic staff help you get checked in and point you in the right direction. You may be asked to confirm your appointment and review forms or history information.

Keep your medicines, records, and questions easy to reach.
03 / While You Wait

While you wait

This is a good moment to gather the details you want to share.

  • what happened before, during, and after the event
  • medicines you take now
  • questions you want answered
  • a family member or friend who saw what happened
04 / Meet The Doctor

Meet your seizure doctor

An epileptologist is a neurologist who specializes in seizures. The visit is a conversation, not a test you can fail.

"I'm here to listen and work with you."
05 / Your Story

Your story matters

Your doctor will ask what you felt, what other people saw, how long it lasted, and how you felt afterward. Even small details can help.

If a family member or friend witnessed the event, their description can be useful too.
06 / Before, During, After

Before, during, after

Before, during, and after details help explain the event.

07 / Which Details Matter

Which details matter

You do not need perfect memory. Just share the parts you remember and ask family or friends to help fill in what they saw.

Before Warning feelings can include a strange smell or taste, a rising feeling, deja vu, fear, or just a sudden sense that something is wrong.
During Tell your doctor what you did and what others saw, such as staring, shaking, stiffening, falling, or not responding.
After Recovery details matter too: confusion, exhaustion, soreness, headache, or trouble speaking can all be useful clues.
Important Not everyone has all three. Share the parts that happened to you.
08 / Full Story

The full story helps

Your doctor is piecing together more than one moment. The event itself, the history around it, and your daily life and support system can all help explain what happened.

A more complete story can make the next steps clearer.
09 / Full Story

What fills in the full story

Your doctor may also ask about your history, daily life, and other details that affect safety and care.

  • Backstory: Family history, early events, past head injury, prior tests, and medicines tried before.
  • Daily life: Driving, work or school, sleep, stress, support at home, and alcohol or other substances.
  • Private topics: Some questions are asked one-on-one when they affect safe care, including pregnancy planning when relevant.
10 / What May Happen Today

What may happen today

The first visit often includes a careful history, a neurologic exam, and a discussion about next steps. Your doctor may talk about tests such as an EEG, MRI, or blood work.

History A detailed narrative history of your seizure, including what you felt, what others observed, how long it lasted, and how you recovered.
Exam A neurologic exam may be part of the visit.
Tests EEG, MRI, or blood work may be discussed.
Timing Not every test happens the same day.
11 / Making Sense

One seizure does not automatically mean epilepsy

A seizure is a sudden burst of electrical activity in the brain. Having one seizure does not automatically mean epilepsy. This visit is about understanding what happened and what still needs to be checked next.

12 / Questions

Bring your questions

Questions help you leave with a clearer plan.

  • Can I drive?
  • What should I do if this happens again?
  • Do I need testing right away?
  • When should I call the clinic?
  • What should my family watch for?
13 / Before You Leave

Leave with a plan

Before you go, you should understand the next step: testing, medicines if needed, safety guidance, and follow-up.

"You came in with questions. You should leave knowing what happens next."

You do not have to figure this out alone

Bring story. Bring questions. Bring support. Ask for clarity. This visit is the start of a plan, not the end of the story.
